# Seasonality Guide

## Why Seasonality is a Real Edge

Seasonality does not predict what *will* happen — it tells you what has *tended* to happen across decades and hundreds of data points. It's a probabilistic tailwind or headwind on top of your technical and fundamental analysis. Trading against strong seasonal headwinds is like paddling upstream: possible, but you're working harder for the same result.

Key caveat: seasonality is most reliable when combined with technical structure and market regime. A bearish September in a bull market is very different from a bearish September in a bear market.

## Key Seasonal Patterns — Evidence and Depth

### January Effect: Small Caps Outperform
**Historical data (1927–2021):** Small stocks outperformed large stocks in January by 2.1% on average.
**Recent trend (2002–2021):** The outperformance shrank to 0.4% — statistically insignificant.
**Verdict:** The January Effect is real in historical data but has been arbitraged away. With lower trading costs and ETFs enabling easy small-cap exposure in December anticipation, the edge has mostly disappeared. Use it as a *slight lean* toward small caps (IWM/Russell 2000) in January, not a primary strategy.
**Exception:** In January 2026, Russell 2000 was up 9.5% vs S&P 500 +1.0% — the pattern does still appear, just inconsistently.

### Sell in May: Myth or Edge?
**Data:** Since 1950, S&P 500 averages +7.2% from November–April vs +2.1% from May–October.
**Not a myth.** The weak-half effect is statistically robust over 70+ years. However, it does not mean "exit everything in May" — it means reduce exposure, raise cash, tighten stops, and be more selective.
**Practical approach:** After April earnings season, reduce net long exposure by 20-30%. Stay long in strong relative-strength names. Avoid adding new speculative positions.

### Summer Doldrums: July–August
- **July** is the exception — Q2 earnings lifts it to a +1.2% average. Best summer month.
- **August** historically averages -0.1%, 51% win rate. Low volume, thin markets, whipsaw action.
- **Strategy:** In July, play earnings momentum. In August, reduce size, sell premium (low VIX = cheap premium but market historically choppy). Avoid chasing moves — they often reverse with no volume to sustain them.

### September Curse: The Worst Month
**Data:** Avg return -0.6% since 1950, only negative-average month in the calendar. S&P 500 ended lower 56% of Septembers since 1928.
**Why it happens (multiple causes working together):**
1. **Mutual fund fiscal year end (Sept 30):** Funds purge losers and rebalance, creating concentrated selling.
2. **Portfolio rebalancing after summer:** Managers return from vacation and reset positions — selling the summer's winners.
3. **Bond issuance spike:** Bond deals that were delayed over summer flood the market in September, pulling capital from equities.
4. **Federal fiscal year end (Sept 30):** Budget debates, debt ceiling anxiety, potential shutdown risks create uncertainty.
**Strategy:** Stay cautious through September. Hedge existing longs with puts or reduce size. Look for the setup into October — October has historically been a major low-making month before Q4 rallies.

### Q4 Rally: October–December
**Data:** October through December averages collectively ~4% with November (+1.6%) and December (+1.4%) being the two strongest months.
**Santa Claus Rally (last 5 trading days of Dec + first 2 of Jan):** Positive 78% of the time since 1950.
**Why Q4 works:** Window dressing by fund managers (buying winners to look good in year-end statements), tax-loss selling exhaustion by mid-December, holiday optimism, reduced selling pressure, and January effect anticipation.
**Strategy:** October can be volatile early (watch for "October Panic" setups — often great buying opportunities). November and December are highest-probability long months. Add exposure in October dips.

### Quad Witching: Four Times a Year
The **third Friday of March, June, September, and December** sees the simultaneous expiration of stock index futures, options on stock index futures, single-stock options, and stock index options.

**2026 Dates:** March 20, June 19, September 18, December 18

**Data and impact:**
- Trading volume spikes 50–100% above normal
- In March 2025, ~$4.7 trillion in derivatives expired in a single session — the highest S&P 500 volume day of that year
- Intraday price swings can be 50–100% larger than typical days
- The week before Quad Witching has historically been bullish as dealers hedge. The day itself (especially the final hour) tends toward selling as positions unwind
- September Quad Witching in 2023 averaged a 1.22% decline on the day itself

**Strategy:** The week *before* Quad Witching can be traded long. The day itself — reduce exposure or stand aside in the final hour. Use elevated volume to exit positions you've been unable to unwind.

### Tax-Loss Selling and January Bounce
**December pattern:** Losing positions are sold in December (especially first half) for tax purposes. This creates artificial selling pressure on beaten-down stocks.
**January bounce:** That selling pressure reverses — oversold, tax-loss names bounce in January. Look for stocks down 30–50% year-to-date in November/December as January bounce candidates. Buy in late December after selling exhausts.

### Earnings Seasons: IV Expansion and Crush
Four earnings seasons per year: January (Q4), April (Q1), July (Q2), October (Q3).
**IV Expansion play:** Buy an ATM straddle or calls 10–14 days before earnings when IV is still relatively low. Sell 1 day before earnings when IV has peaked. Captures the volatility expansion without taking the binary risk of the earnings event itself.
**IV Crush reality:** Front-month IV commonly drops 30–60% immediately after earnings. A stock with IV at 80% heading into earnings might settle at 30–35% the next morning — even after a 5–10% actual move. This means buying options the day before earnings usually loses even if direction is correct.
**Sell volatility approach:** Iron condors, short straddles, and credit spreads positioned before earnings capture the IV crush as the edge. Requires accurate range estimation.

## Crypto Seasonality

Data from CoinGlass, Barchart, and Quantpedia crypto studies (available from ~2013):

| Month | BTC Avg Return | Bias | Notes |
|-------|---------------|------|-------|
| January | +25-30% | Bullish | Tax refunds, new year positioning, fresh capital |
| February | +15-20% | Bullish | Post-January momentum often continues |
| March | +5-10% | Neutral-bullish | Mixed; can see corrections after Jan-Feb rally |
| April | +20%+ | Bullish | Historically one of the strongest months |
| May | -5 to -10% | Bearish | "Crypto May" mirrors equity weakness |
| June | -10 to -15% | Bearish | Among the weakest months for BTC |
| July | +15-20% | Bullish | Relief rally; one of strongest months in data |
| August | -0.5% | Neutral-bearish | Weakest summer month |
| September | -4.2% | **Worst month** | September effect extends to crypto; avg -4.16% |
| October | +20-30% | **Strongest** | "Uptober" — avg +24%, historically the strongest |
| November | +35-40% | Very strong | Average +37.51% — highest average of any month |
| December | +15-20% | Bullish | Year-end rally, continuation from Oct-Nov |

**"Uptober" and Q4:** BTC's October average gain of ~24-30% and November's ~37% are extraordinary. This aligns with equity Q4 strength but is amplified in crypto.
**September is bad in BOTH markets:** The September effect extends to crypto. BTC averages -4.16% in September — more negative than equities. This is not a coincidence; it reflects the same institutional rebalancing and risk-off dynamics.
**Halving year patterns:** Post-halving years (2013, 2017, 2021, 2025) have historically seen the strongest Q4 bull runs. The 12-18 months after a halving tend to be the cycle's strongest period.

## VIX Seasonality (Options Pricing Calendar)

The CBOE VIX tracks expected 30-day volatility in S&P 500 options. Its seasonal pattern matters for options traders:

| Period | VIX Tendency | Implication |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| June–July | **Lowest VIX** — summer calm | Options are *cheapest*. Buy protection (puts/VIX calls) while cheap. Sell premium expensive relative to realized vol. |
| August | Rising from summer lows | Begin reducing naked short volatility exposure |
| September–October | **Highest spike risk** | Most dangerous. VIX's all-time high was October 23, 2008 (96.40). October 1987, 2008, 2020 all saw massive VIX spikes. |
| November–December | VIX typically retreats | Holiday calm, but watch for year-end position squaring |
| January–March | Moderate, often rising | Election years and macro uncertainty can keep VIX elevated |

**Key rule:** The best time to buy insurance (puts, VIX calls) is when the VIX is calm (summer). The worst time is after a spike. Buy the umbrella before the storm.

## Seasonality vs. Reality: When to Override

Seasonality is a **probability** tool, not a certainty. Override seasonal bias when:
1. The macro regime is strongly opposed (e.g., September in a roaring bull market after major catalyst)
2. The technical structure is clearly contradicting the seasonal expectation
3. A major fundamental event overrides the seasonal effect (Fed pivot, major earnings, geopolitical shock)

**Seasonality adds 10-15% to your edge, at best.** It does not replace trend analysis, technicals, or risk management. Use it to:
- Confirm a trade you already like
- Set position size (lean larger when seasonality agrees)
- Set expectations for volatility
- Time entries (buy September dips for October recovery)
