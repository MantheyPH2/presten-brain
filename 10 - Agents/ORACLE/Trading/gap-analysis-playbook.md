# Gap Analysis Playbook

## What a Gap Is and Why It Matters

A gap occurs when a stock or index opens significantly above or below the prior day's close, creating a "gap" in the chart with no trading activity. Gaps are meaningful because they represent conviction — someone was so compelled to buy or sell that they did not wait for a more favorable price. The nature of that conviction (legitimate catalyst vs. overreaction vs. manipulation) determines whether the gap holds or fills.

**Key truth:** Not all gaps are equal. The single most important question about any gap is: *Is there a legitimate, volume-backed catalyst?* Everything else flows from the answer.

---

## The Four Types of Gaps

### 1. Common Gap
- **What it is:** A gap on a normal day with no specific catalyst. Often happens in low-liquidity conditions, weekends, or within a consolidating range.
- **Volume:** Low — volume does not confirm the gap.
- **What it does:** Fills approximately 90% of the time, usually within 1–3 trading days.
- **Significance:** Low. Do not build a directional trade based on a common gap. The fill trade (fading the gap back to prior close) is often the better play.
- **Example:** Stock closes at $50, opens at $51.50 with no news, lower-than-average pre-market volume.

### 2. Breakaway Gap
- **What it is:** A gap that breaks out of a defined consolidation range or key technical level, usually accompanied by a major catalyst.
- **Volume:** High — 2x or more average daily volume.
- **What it does:** Fills less than 30% of the time within the first week. The gap represents a structural shift in the stock's valuation or sentiment.
- **Significance:** High. Trade in the direction of the gap. The breakout level (where the gap started) becomes new support (for up gaps) or resistance (for down gaps).
- **Example:** Biotech stock in a 3-month range at $30–35 reports Phase 3 success, gaps to $55 on 5x volume. The gap likely holds.

### 3. Runaway / Continuation Gap
- **What it is:** A gap that occurs in the middle of an established, strong trend. Represents a surge in momentum, often triggered by a secondary catalyst or momentum acceleration.
- **Volume:** High, sustained.
- **What it does:** Confirms the trend continues. Measured move target = distance already traveled from trend start. Does not typically fill quickly.
- **Significance:** Medium-High. Signals the trend has more room. Do not fade. Entry on any pullback to the gap level.
- **Example:** Stock already up 40% over 3 months gaps up another 8% on analyst upgrade — the uptrend is accelerating.

### 4. Exhaustion Gap
- **What it is:** A gap at the *end* of a trend, often after an extended move. Appears bullish but is actually a final burst of buying (or selling) before the trend reverses.
- **Volume:** Very high initially — then volume dries up rapidly as the gap fails to hold.
- **What it does:** Often fills within days. The volume spike without follow-through is the tell.
- **Significance:** High (as reversal signal). When you see a gap after a long move, with high volume but price then stalls and reverses intraday — that's exhaustion.
- **Example:** Stock up 80% in 2 months gaps up another 10% on good news, opens at the high, then slowly grinds down all day, closes near the low of the gap candle on huge volume.

## Gap Statistics (Research Data)

From multiple studies including analysis of 6,005 QQQ trading days (March 1999–February 2023) and S&P 500 gap research:

### Overall Fill Rates
- **All gaps, eventually:** ~70–76% of all gaps close at some point during regular trading hours (lifetime basis)
- **S&P 500 gaps, recent (6-month window):** ~60% fill rate
- **Common gaps specifically:** ~90% fill within 1–3 trading days

### Fill Rate by Gap Size
| Gap Size | Day 1 Fill Rate | Day 2 Fill Rate | Notes |
|----------|---------------|---------------|-------|
| 0.5%–0.99% | 59% | 74% | Small gaps almost always fill quickly |
| 1.0%–1.99% | 47% | 57% | Medium gaps, fill within 2 days slightly more than half |
| 2.0%–4.9% | ~35% | ~45% | Larger gaps require a catalyst absence to fill |
| 5%+ | ~25% | ~35% | Significant gaps driven by real news rarely fill quickly |

### Fill Rate by Volume
| Volume vs Average | 2-Day Fill Rate | Interpretation |
|------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Low volume gap | 85% | Weak conviction — expect fill |
| Average volume gap | 55–65% | Neutral — evaluate catalyst |
| High volume gap (2x+) | 30–45% | Strong conviction — lean toward continuation |

### Gap Direction
- **Down gaps fill more often than up gaps.** Bear gaps (stock opens lower) are more frequently reversed than bull gaps. This may reflect the asymmetric tendency of markets to recover from fear-driven selling more quickly than from excitement-driven buying.

### Earnings Gaps Specifically
- **Earnings gap up with volume:** Continuation over the next 2–3 days is the more common outcome. The initial gap is followed by institutional accumulation as funds that missed the move buy the open.
- **Earnings gap down:** More complex. Initial gap often overshoots fair value (panic selling), leading to a partial fill after 2–3 days. But the underlying bad news (guidance cut, margin miss) often keeps the stock depressed for weeks.
- **Gap and Go success rate with strong catalyst:** 60–70% win rate when combined with right float, volume, and clear fundamental catalyst. Without catalyst: success rate drops to ~40%.

## Gap Trading Strategies

### Strategy 1: Gap and Go (Momentum)

**Setup requirements:**
- Gap of 3%+ in either direction
- Clear, strong catalyst (earnings beat, M&A, regulatory approval, major upgrade)
- Volume: Pre-market and opening volume must be 2x+ the stock's average daily volume
- Float: Works best on stocks with float under 50M shares (more volatile, cleaner moves)

**Execution:**
1. Identify the gap pre-market. Confirm the catalyst is real (not rumor, not already known).
2. Mark the pre-market high (PMH) and pre-market low (PML) on your chart.
3. Wait for the first **5–15 minutes** of regular session trading to establish the opening range.
4. Do not chase the open — let the stock breathe and find its first pullback.
5. Enter on the first pullback bounce: buy when price dips to the VWAP or to the opening range low and begins to recover with volume.
6. Stop: Below the opening range low (or below the first pullback's low). Tight — usually 0.5–1.5% from entry.
7. Target: The measured gap extension. If stock gapped from $50 to $55 (a $5 gap), first target is $57.50 (half the gap added to open), full target is $60 (1x the gap added to open).

**Do not enter if:**
- Volume is fading by 9:45am (gap is losing momentum)
- Price is below VWAP and cannot reclaim it
- Gap is more than 15% (too extended, risk/reward distorts)
- The news is soft (analyst upgrades, sector moves, rumor-based)

### Strategy 2: Gap Fill (Mean Reversion / Fading)

**Setup requirements:**
- Gap of any size with NO clear catalyst — or a gap on soft news that does not justify the move
- Pre-market volume is low or below average
- The gap is into resistance (for up gaps) or into support (for down gaps)

**Execution:**
1. Identify the gap and confirm there is no legitimate news driving it.
2. Mark the prior day's close — this is your fill target.
3. Wait for the opening 5 minutes. If price immediately continues in the gap direction with volume, do not fade — the gap may be a breakaway.
4. If price stalls, fades, or reverses in the first 5–15 minutes → initiate fade position.
5. Direction: Against the gap (short an up gap, long a down gap).
6. Stop: Above the pre-market high (for short) or below the pre-market low (for long). This defines if the gap has real conviction.
7. Target: Prior day's close (the gap fill). Partial profit at 50% of fill; full target at complete fill.

**Gap Fill edge conditions:**
- August and low-volume summer sessions: gap fills most likely (market is thin, moves exaggerate)
- No news + small gap + low volume = 85% fill probability per historical data
- Gap fills are most reliable in the first 2 hours of the session

### Strategy 3: Earnings Gap — Direction Plays

**Earnings gap up (with volume and guidance raise):**
- Do not chase the open. The stock often dips in the first 30–60 minutes as early buyers take profits.
- Buy the first pullback to the VWAP with volume confirmation.
- Target: Add another leg equal to the earnings gap over the next 2–3 sessions.
- Stop: Below the earnings day low.
- Note: Institutional accumulation typically continues for 2–3 days after a major earnings beat as underweight funds add to positions.

**Earnings gap down (with bad guidance):**
- Day 1–2: Do NOT buy the dip. Oversold does not mean finished selling. Let the dust settle.
- Day 2–3: Look for capitulation (high volume day, closes above the low, potential hammer candle).
- Day 3+: Consider a mean reversion bounce trade — target 30–50% of the gap fill.
- Stop: New low below the earnings day close.
- Avoid: Stocks with structural problems (revenue decline, fraud risk, sector headwind). Bad earnings often lead to sustained downtrends, not bounces.

## Key Rules to Internalize

1. **Volume is the judge.** A gap on low volume is almost always a fade candidate. A gap on massive volume respects itself.
2. **Never trade the open tick.** The first 5–15 minutes of a gap day are chaos. Let the open establish. Wait for your setup.
3. **The prior close is magnetic.** Approximately 70–76% of all gaps eventually fill. Price gravitates back to where trading last happened.
4. **Context transforms the trade.** A 5% gap in a downtrending stock on a bad day is different from a 5% gap in an uptrending stock on a strong market day. The market environment matters.
5. **Identify the trap.** The most dangerous scenario is a gap that looks like a breakaway but is actually exhaustion. Check: Is this after an extended move? Is volume fading after the initial spike? Are sellers stepping in immediately at the open? That's the trap.
