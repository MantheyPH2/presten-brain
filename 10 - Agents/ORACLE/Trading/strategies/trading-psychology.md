# Trading Psychology: How to Stop Losing Money

## The Core Problem

Trading is a game played against your own brain. Every cognitive bias evolution gave you for survival works AGAINST you in markets. Fear, greed, loss aversion, pattern-seeking -- all adaptive in the wild, all destructive in trading.

**The gap between knowing and doing is where most traders fail.** You can memorize every strategy in this vault and still lose money if you can't manage your psychology.

---

## The 7 Deadly Sins of Trading

### 1. FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
**What it looks like**: Chasing a stock that already moved 20% because you didn't want to miss the next 20%.

**Why it happens**: Social proof + regret aversion. You see others making money and your brain interprets missing the trade as a loss.

**The fix**:
- Accept that you will miss trades. There will always be another one.
- If you didn't plan the trade, don't take the trade.
- Rule: If you're chasing, you're late. The best entries come from patience, not urgency.
- Keep a "missed trades" log. Review it monthly. You'll find that most "missed" trades would have been losses anyway.

### 2. Revenge Trading
**What it looks like**: After a loss, immediately taking another trade to "win it back." Usually with larger size. Usually results in a larger loss.

**Why it happens**: Loss aversion triggers an emotional response. Your brain wants to eliminate the pain of the loss as fast as possible.

**The fix**:
- **Daily loss limit: 2%.** Hit it and you're done. Close the platform. Walk away.
- After any loss, wait 15 minutes minimum before your next trade.
- Ask: "Would I take this trade if I hadn't just lost?" If no, don't take it.
- The next trade has NOTHING to do with the last trade. Each trade is independent.

### 3. Overconfidence (After Winning Streaks)
**What it looks like**: After 5 wins in a row, increasing position size dramatically. "I can't lose." Then one outsized loss wipes out all 5 wins.

**Why it happens**: Recency bias. Your brain extrapolates recent success into the future.

**The fix**:
- Never increase position size by more than 25% after a winning streak.
- Better: Keep position size CONSTANT regardless of recent results.
- Review: "Am I sizing this based on my system's rules, or based on how I feel?"
- The market doesn't know or care about your winning streak.

### 4. Loss Aversion (Not Cutting Losses)
**What it looks like**: The stock hits your stop loss... but you move the stop. "It'll come back." It doesn't.

**Why it happens**: Losses feel 2x more painful than equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman's Prospect Theory). Moving the stop delays the pain.

**The fix**:
- Stops are placed BEFORE entry and NEVER moved further away. This is a hard rule.
- Mental reframe: "My stop loss is protecting me. It's my friend, not my enemy."
- The loss is already determined when you enter the trade. The stop loss is just executing a decision you already made.
- Pre-accept the loss before entering: "I am risking $X on this trade. If I lose $X, that's the cost of doing business."

### 5. Confirmation Bias
**What it looks like**: You're bullish on a stock. You only look for reasons it'll go up. You ignore every bearish signal.

**Why it happens**: Your brain seeks information that confirms existing beliefs and filters out contradictory information.

**The fix**:
- Before every trade, list 3 reasons it could fail.
- Actively seek the opposing view. If you're bullish, look for bears. What do they see?
- Have a trading buddy who challenges your thesis.
- If you can't find a single reason the trade could fail, your analysis is incomplete.

### 6. Anchoring
**What it looks like**: "I bought at $100. I won't sell until it gets back to $100." The stock is at $60 and falling.

**Why it happens**: Your brain anchors to the purchase price as the "real" value of the asset. The market doesn't care what you paid.

**The fix**:
- The current price is the only price that matters. Your entry price is irrelevant to where the stock is going.
- Ask: "If I had no position, would I buy this stock at the current price?" If no, sell.
- Cost basis should affect your tax decision, not your trading decision.

### 7. Overtrading
**What it looks like**: Taking 15 trades a day when your system calls for 2-3. Trading because you're bored, not because there's a setup.

**Why it happens**: Action bias. Sitting and waiting feels like you're "not doing anything." Your brain equates activity with progress.

**The fix**:
- Set a maximum number of trades per day (3 for day traders, 1-2 for swing)
- If you've hit your limit, close the platform. You cannot trade.
- Trading rewards patience, not activity. The best traders do LESS, not more.
- Quality of setups > Quantity of trades. Always.

## Building a Trading Mindset

### Think in Probabilities
No single trade matters. What matters is the next 100 trades. Any single trade can win or lose regardless of how good the setup is. Focus on executing your system across hundreds of trades and the edge will manifest.

### Process Over Outcomes
- A good trade that loses money is still a good trade (if you followed your rules)
- A bad trade that makes money is still a bad trade (if you broke your rules)
- Judge your trading by process adherence, not P&L

### Emotional Neutrality
The goal: you don't get happy when you win, and you don't get angry when you lose. You just execute the plan.

This sounds robotic. It is. The best traders ARE robotic in their execution. Emotions are for life. Trading is for systems.

## Practical Tools

### 1. Trading Journal (Non-Negotiable)
Every trade entry includes:
- Setup name and timeframe
- Entry/exit prices and sizes
- Emotional state at entry (calm? anxious? excited? FOMO?)
- Rule adherence score (1-10: did you follow every rule?)
- Post-trade review: What did you learn?

Review weekly. Look for patterns: "I lose most when I trade out of boredom" or "My FOMO trades have a 25% win rate."

### 2. Pre-Trade Checklist
Before every trade, run through:
- [ ] Does this match a setup from my playbook?
- [ ] Is the R:R at least 1.5:1?
- [ ] Have I placed my stop loss?
- [ ] Am I within my daily/portfolio risk limits?
- [ ] Am I calm and rational, or emotional?
- [ ] Would I take this trade if I'd just had 3 losses in a row?

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure," don't take the trade.

### 3. The 10-Second Rule
When you feel the urge to enter a trade impulsively, wait 10 seconds and take 3 deep breaths. If you still want to take it, check your pre-trade checklist. If it passes, take it. If it doesn't, walk away.

## The Mark Douglas Framework

From "Trading in the Zone" (the single best trading psychology book):

1. **Anything can happen** in any single trade
2. **You don't need to know** what's going to happen to make money
3. **There is a random distribution** between wins and losses for any given set of variables that define an edge
4. **An edge is nothing more** than a higher probability of one thing happening over another
5. **Every moment in the market is unique**

These five truths, internalized, transform your relationship with uncertainty and risk.
