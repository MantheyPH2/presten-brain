# The Wyckoff Method

## What It Is

The Wyckoff Method is a technical analysis framework developed by Richard D. Wyckoff in the early 1900s. It explains how markets move through repeating cycles driven by institutional accumulation and distribution. Despite being over 100 years old, it remains one of the most powerful frameworks for understanding market structure because the underlying mechanism hasn't changed: large players still need to accumulate and distribute positions.

---

## Three Laws of Wyckoff

### 1. Law of Supply and Demand
- Price rises when demand > supply
- Price falls when supply > demand  
- Price moves sideways when supply = demand
- **Practical use**: Volume confirms which side is winning

### 2. Law of Cause and Effect
- The time spent in a trading range (accumulation/distribution) = the "cause"
- The resulting trend = the "effect"
- **Bigger cause = bigger effect**: A longer accumulation period leads to a larger markup
- **Practical use**: Measure the width of the trading range to estimate the magnitude of the coming move (point & figure count)

### 3. Law of Effort vs. Result
- Volume (effort) should match price movement (result)
- If high volume produces little price change = absorption/reversal incoming
- If low volume accompanies a price move = the move lacks conviction
- **Practical use**: Volume/price divergences are your best friend

## The Composite Man

Wyckoff's central concept: think of the market as if ONE entity (the "Composite Man") is behind all the action. This entity:
- Accumulates at lows before marking up
- Distributes at highs before marking down  
- Creates false moves to trap retail traders
- Operates with a plan and purpose

**Your job**: Study the Composite Man's behavior and trade in alignment with it. This maps directly to modern Smart Money Concepts.

---

## The Four Market Phases

### Phase 1: Accumulation
Smart money quietly buys at low prices. Price moves sideways in a range.

### Phase 2: Markup
Once accumulation is complete, price breaks out and trends upward. Volume expands on advances.

### Phase 3: Distribution
Smart money sells to retail at high prices. Price moves sideways in a range.

### Phase 4: Markdown
Once distribution is complete, price breaks down and trends downward.

---

## Accumulation Schematic (The Buying Phase)

### Events in Order:

**PS (Preliminary Support)**: First sign that buying interest is entering. Volume increases on a down move but price stabilizes. Not a buy signal -- just a warning that the downtrend may be ending.

**SC (Selling Climax)**: Panic selling on extreme volume. Price drops sharply and closes near the lows. The "capitulation" moment. Smart money absorbs all this selling. This often marks the bottom of the range.

**AR (Automatic Rally)**: After the SC, price bounces sharply on reduced selling pressure. This sets the upper boundary of the trading range.

**ST (Secondary Test)**: Price retests the SC area on lower volume. If volume and spread are reduced, it confirms that selling pressure is exhausted. This confirms the range.

**Phase B -- Building the Cause**: Price oscillates between the SC low and AR high. Smart money is accumulating positions. Multiple STs may occur. Volume gives clues -- look for higher volume on rallies and lower volume on dips.

**Spring / Shakeout**: THE KEY EVENT. Price dips below the SC/ST lows, triggering stop losses and scaring retail traders into selling. Smart money absorbs this selling. Price quickly reverses back into the range.

Three types of springs:
- **Spring #1**: Deep penetration, high volume. May need another test.
- **Spring #2**: Moderate penetration. The classic tradeable spring.
- **Spring #3**: Shallow penetration. Hardest to catch but most bullish.

**Test of the Spring**: After the spring, price should pull back on low volume. This confirms that supply has been absorbed. THIS is your entry point.

**SOS (Sign of Strength)**: Price rallies on increased volume and wide spread, breaking above recent resistance within the range. Confirms that demand is in control.

**LPS (Last Point of Support)**: A pullback after SOS on declining volume. This is another entry point -- the last good price before markup begins.

**BU (Back-Up)**: Price may pull back to the top of the range one more time. Final entry opportunity.

## Distribution Schematic (The Selling Phase)

Mirror image of accumulation:

**PSY (Preliminary Supply)**: First sign that selling is entering after an uptrend.

**BC (Buying Climax)**: Euphoric buying on extreme volume. Price spikes on a wide-range bar. Smart money begins selling into this demand.

**AR (Automatic Reaction)**: Sharp pullback after BC. Sets the lower boundary of the distribution range.

**ST (Secondary Test)**: Price retests the BC area on lower volume. Confirms the range.

**Phase B**: Price oscillates. Smart money distributes (sells) throughout this phase.

**UTAD (Upthrust After Distribution)**: THE KEY EVENT. Price breaks above the BC/ST highs, trapping breakout buyers. Smart money sells into this demand. Price reverses sharply back into the range.

**LPSY (Last Point of Supply)**: Weak rally attempts on low volume. Shows that demand is exhausted.

**SOW (Sign of Weakness)**: Price breaks below support on increased volume. Confirms supply is in control.

**Markdown begins**: Price trends downward as distribution is complete.

## How to Trade Wyckoff

### Accumulation Entry Strategy
1. Identify a potential accumulation range (selling climax + automatic rally)
2. Watch for secondary tests on declining volume
3. Wait for the spring (price drops below range on low volume, then recovers)
4. Enter on the test of the spring (pullback on low volume)
5. Stop loss below the spring low
6. Target: measure the width of the range and project upward (cause = effect)

### Distribution Entry Strategy
1. Identify a potential distribution range (buying climax + automatic reaction)
2. Watch for secondary tests on declining volume
3. Wait for the upthrust/UTAD (price breaks above range, then reverses)
4. Enter short on the LPSY (weak rally on low volume)
5. Stop loss above the UTAD high
6. Target: measure the width of the range and project downward

## Practical Tips

- **Wyckoff works on all timeframes** but is most reliable on Daily and 4H
- **Volume is essential** -- you cannot trade Wyckoff without volume analysis
- **Not every range is accumulation/distribution** -- some are just consolidation. Volume behavior tells you which.
- **The spring/upthrust is the highest-probability entry** but also the most psychologically difficult (you're buying at a new low / selling at a new high)
- **Wyckoff + SMC alignment** = extremely high-probability setups (spring = liquidity sweep, SOS = break of structure)
