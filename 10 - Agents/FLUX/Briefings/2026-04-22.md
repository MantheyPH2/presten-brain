## Research Findings — Competitive Intelligence Highlights

**Gymshark's decay is Exel Labs' opportunity:**
Gymshark scaled to $600M by building a community of serious gym-goers, then genericized as it chased volume. Their early customer base is now 28–35, earning real money, and looking for something that has kept pace with them. Exel Labs is positioned to be that upgrade brand.

**Represent Clo proves the model:**
A UK brand doing athletic-streetwear at $100–$260 price points has carved out $60–80M in revenue and growing. The US doesn't have a clean version of this for the gym-trained male. This is the template — not to copy, but to use as proof of concept for the positioning.

**The margin math works:**
At an $85–90 average selling price with $28–32 COGS landed, gross margins of 62–65% are achievable. This is on par with Lululemon and Vuori. The unit economics are excellent if customer acquisition cost is managed through organic and seeded growth rather than paid advertising in the early stage.

**Influencer seeding still works — but only if done right:**
Every successful DTC athletic brand (Gymshark, Alphalete, Ten Thousand) built its initial customer base through seeded organic content, not paid ads. The pattern is consistent. The difference is selectivity — Gymshark sent to 6 YouTubers in 2014, not 600. Quality of fit matters more than reach.
